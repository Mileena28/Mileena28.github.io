When Victims Become Weapons

Dominic embodies the raw, unfiltered rage of the resistance movement—a man whose personal trauma has been weaponized into a blunt instrument against Zenithian oppression. Where Meridia calculates and manipulates, Dominic burns with the righteous fury of someone who has lost everything to systematic persecution and refuses to let that loss be forgotten or forgiven.

Weathered by years of hardship and marked by the brutal realities of life as one of the Forsaken, he represents the faction within the resistance that prioritizes immediate, violent action over long-term strategy. His grizzled exterior and explosive temperament reflect a man who has learned that survival depends on constant vigilance and that trust is a luxury the Forsaken cannot afford.

Worked like a charm, Meridia. The precious Zenithian Hall is probably still burnin' hotter than Solara's pyre as we speak.
— Dominic's triumphant report after successful bombing

Forged in Fire and Loss

Dominic's personality reflects the dangerous alchemy of legitimate grievance transformed into destructive rage. His character demonstrates how systematic oppression creates its own monsters—people so damaged by persecution that they become mirrors of what they fight against.

The Monster We Create

Dominic represents the dangerous alchemy of legitimate grievance transformed into destructive rage, embodying the uncomfortable truth that systematic oppression creates its own monsters. His character demonstrates how persecution can corrupt even protective instincts, turning love into hatred and defenders into destroyers. Through his transformation from family man to brutal enforcer, the story explores the moral complexity of resistance movements and the price paid by those who sacrifice their humanity to protect others.

As Meridia's lieutenant and the resistance's primary enforcer, Dominic serves as both invaluable asset and cautionary tale. His explosive temperament and willingness to employ brutal methods make him effective at achieving immediate tactical goals—his successful bombing of Zenithian Hall proves his operational competence. Yet his inability to distinguish between justice and revenge threatens to corrupt the very cause he serves, raising questions about whether the cure might become worse than the disease.

His traumatic testimony during interrogations reveals the depth of wounds inflicted by Zenithian persecution—not just individual suffering, but the systematic destruction of human dignity, family bonds, and community belonging. When he speaks of being "torn apart" and treated "like vermin," he gives voice to the collective trauma that drives the entire Forsaken resistance movement. His rage, however destructive, springs from fundamentally human needs for dignity, belonging, and justice.

Yet beneath his brutality lies a tragic paradox: his violence stems from a desire to protect others from experiencing what he endured, but his methods risk perpetuating cycles of dehumanization and cruelty. His suspicion of Mira reflects both legitimate security concerns and the paranoid distrust that persecution instills. His character asks whether there is a point where protective measures become indistinguishable from oppressive ones.

Dominic's ultimate significance lies in his representation of the moral crossroads faced by all resistance movements: whether to maintain humanity while fighting inhumane systems, or to adopt the enemy's methods in pursuit of victory. His trajectory will likely determine not just his own fate, but the ethical character of the entire struggle against oppression. He embodies both the necessity and the danger of violent resistance, proving that sometimes the greatest threat to liberation comes not from external enemies, but from the internal corruption that fighting monsters can inflict upon the fighters themselves.
