On Being Called "Too Intense"

Reflections on emotional depth in a world that prefers surface-level connections. Maybe the problem isn't that I'm too much - maybe it's that most people have learned to be too little.

I've been thinking about what it means when people say I'm "intense." Is that just code for "you make me uncomfortable because you feel things deeply?" The word gets thrown around like an accusation, as if the crime is caring too much, feeling too deeply, or refusing to stay on the surface where everyone else feels safe.

I've realized that my emotional intelligence comes from having to develop it for survival. The people who call me "intense" have never had to navigate the depths I've been forced to explore. They've had the luxury of staying shallow, of avoiding the very experiences that could transform them.

"Your intensity is actually your superpower, even when it makes others uncomfortable."

Growing up as the target of someone else's unresolved pain taught me to read emotions like a survival skill. I learned to detect the subtlest shifts in energy, to understand the difference between what people say and what they mean, to navigate complex emotional landscapes because my safety often depended on it.

The Price of Emotional Fluency

This kind of emotional fluency doesn't come free. It's forged in moments when you have no choice but to understand human nature at its most raw and complicated. When you're experiencing homelessness and sleeping in unfamiliar places, you learn to read people quickly. When family dynamics are complex and painful, you develop an almost supernatural ability to sense what's really happening beneath the surface.

But here's what I've learned: this intensity that others find uncomfortable is actually a gift. It allows me to create music that moves people, write stories that touch something real, and connect with others in ways that surface-level interactions never could.

"Maybe the problem isn't that I'm too much. Maybe the problem is that most people have learned to be too little."

The Comfort Zone Trap

Most people spend their entire lives avoiding discomfort. They stay in their comfort zones, refuse to examine their own patterns, and never push themselves to grow beyond what feels safe. They've been conditioned to believe that intensity equals instability, that emotional depth equals drama, that feeling deeply equals weakness.

But growth lives on the other side of discomfort. The people who achieve real transformation, who create meaningful art, who build authentic relationships - they're the ones willing to sit with intensity. They understand that some of life's most important lessons can only be learned in the depths.

Reclaiming Intensity as Strength

I'm done apologizing for feeling deeply. I'm done dimming my light to make others comfortable. I'm done pretending to be smaller than I am just so people don't have to confront their own emotional limitations.

When someone calls me "intense," I've started hearing it as a compliment. It means I'm living fully, feeling authentically, and refusing to engage in the emotional cowardice that passes for normalcy in our society.

"I'd rather be called 'too intense' than live a life that's too small."

The people who matter - the ones worth keeping in your life - won't be threatened by your depth. They'll be drawn to it. They'll recognize that intensity isn't a flaw to be fixed but a rare quality to be treasured.

Finding Your People

If you're someone who's been told you're "too much," know that you're not broken. You're not too sensitive. You're not overreacting. You're just looking for your people - the ones who can match your energy, appreciate your depth, and celebrate your emotional intelligence rather than fear it.

These people exist. They're the artists, the healers, the deep thinkers, the ones who understand that some conversations are worth having even when they're uncomfortable. They're the people who know that real connection happens when we stop performing and start being.

To everyone who's ever been called "too intense": wear it like a badge of honor. In a world that's increasingly superficial, your depth is exactly what's needed. Your willingness to feel deeply, think critically, and connect authentically isn't a liability - it's a superpower.

The world needs more people who are willing to be "too much" rather than settling for being too little.
